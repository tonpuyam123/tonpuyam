Current Research

The global cultivation of Cavendish bananas is severely threatened by Fusarium wilt caused by the soil-borne fungus Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. cubense tropical race 4 (TR4). Although breeding TR4-resistant cultivars is constrained by triploidy and sterility, the Taiwan Banana Research Institute (TBRI) has developed somaclonal variants that exhibit stable resistance to TR4. However, the precise mutations and molecular mechanisms underlying this resistance remain unclear.

My research aims to elucidate the genetic basis of TR4 resistance in these TBRI somaclonal variants by identifying causal somatic mutations and key regulatory genes. We discovered that highly resistant variants share common chromosomal deletions that reduce gene copy number and expression. Within these deleted regions, we identified candidate susceptibility genes belonging to the Autoinhibited Ca2+-ATPase (ACA) family.

Using a virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) system, we demonstrated that silencing these ACA genes in susceptible bananas elevates salicylic acid (SA) marker gene expression and attenuates TR4 disease symptoms. These findings suggest that reduced ACA expression enhances SA-mediated immunity and contributes to durable resistance to Fusarium wilt.

Ongoing work includes the development of CRISPR-edited lines targeting these ACA genes for stable modification and the generation of transgenic Arabidopsis lines to investigate their subcellular localization. Future directions will test whether silencing or knockout of these genes in other susceptible banana cultivars can similarly confer resistance to TR4.

Research Interests

  • Molecular basis of disease resistance in crops
  • Fusarium wilt TR4 resistance mechanisms in banana
  • Plant–microbe interactions and host immune signaling
  • Functional genomics, transcriptomics, and structural variation
  • Genome editing and marker development for breeding
